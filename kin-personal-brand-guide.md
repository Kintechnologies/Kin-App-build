## 1. Personal Brand Foundations

### What a Personal Brand Actually Is

A personal brand is not a logo, a color palette, or a tagline on your Instagram bio. It's the answer to one question: **when someone describes you to a friend, what do they say?**

Your personal brand is a recognizable point of view. It's the reason someone watches your video instead of the 500 others that just showed up in their feed. It's not about being polished or performative — it's about being *specific*. The most magnetic creators online aren't the most talented or most credentialed. They're the most recognizable. You see three seconds of their video and you know exactly who they are and what they're about.

### Your Unique Positioning

You sit at an intersection that almost nobody else occupies:

- **Non-technical founder** — you don't code, which makes you relatable to 95% of people who have app ideas but think they can't build them
- **Full-time job** — you're not a trust-fund kid or a crypto bro who "retired at 28." You clock in at a startup, which means your time is real and limited
- **Toddler dad** — the chaos is real, the stakes are personal, and the product you're building (a family coordination app) is something you actually need
- **Building with AI agents** — this is the part that makes people lean in. You've assembled a team of AI agents that build your product while you're at work or putting your kid to bed

That combination is magnetic right now for three reasons. First, AI is the biggest topic in tech and culture — everyone is curious but most people don't understand how to actually use it. Second, the "underdog building something real" story never gets old — people root for you. Third, the dad-with-a-day-job angle makes you deeply relatable to a massive audience that feels like they never have enough time for their ambitions.

### Your "One Sentence"

This is the sentence people use when they tell a friend about you. It needs to be short enough to remember and specific enough to be interesting.

> **"This guy has a 9-to-5 and a toddler, doesn't know how to code, and he's building an entire app using AI agents."**

That's it. That's the sentence. Every piece of content you make should reinforce some part of it. When someone watches three of your videos, this sentence should form in their head automatically.

### Voice and Tone

**How to sound on camera:**

You're talking to a friend at a coffee shop, not presenting at a conference. Imagine you just sat down and your buddy asked "so what's going on with that app thing?" That energy — a little excited, a little tired, completely honest — is your voice.

- **Conversational, not performative.** Don't "present." Talk. Use the words you'd actually use. Say "dude" if you say "dude." Say "I have no idea what I'm doing" if that's true.
- **Authentic, not polished.** You're allowed to stumble over words. You're allowed to be in a messy room. The imperfection is the point — it signals that you're real.
- **Enthusiastic without being hype-y.** You're genuinely excited about what you're building. Let that come through. But don't perform excitement. There's a difference between "I'm actually kind of blown away by what happened today" and "OH MY GOD YOU GUYS WON'T BELIEVE THIS."
- **Self-aware.** You can joke about being in over your head. Self-deprecation (light, not sad) is your friend. "I don't know how to code and I'm building a tech company — what could go wrong?" lands better than pretending you have it all figured out.

## 2. The "One Video, One Idea" Framework

This is the single most important section of this guide. Your instinct is going to be to explain everything at once — your vision, the agents, the tech, the product, the journey, the struggle, the future. **That instinct will kill your content.**

### Why Idea Dumping Doesn't Work

When you try to put your entire story into one video, three things happen. The viewer gets overwhelmed and scrolls away within 5 seconds. You sound scattered because you're rushing through ideas that each deserve their own space. And you burn through all your content in one shot — leaving you with nothing to post tomorrow.

### The Iceberg Method

Think of your story as an iceberg. Each video shows *one small piece* above the waterline. The viewer senses there's more underneath, and that's what makes them follow you — they want to see the rest.

Your entire story — "I'm a dad with a 9-to-5 building an AI-powered family app using AI agents even though I can't code" — is the full iceberg. Here's how you break it into individual videos:

**BAD (one video, everything):**
"I'm building an AI family app with agents while working a 9-to-5 and here's how the agents work and here's the app and here's my vision for the future of family management and oh also I have a toddler and—" *(viewer left 4 seconds ago)*

**GOOD (each line is its own video):**

1. "I have a 9-to-5 and a toddler. I'm also building an app. Here's what my mornings look like."
2. "I don't know how to code. So I hired AI agents to build my app for me."
3. "I woke up this morning and my AI agents had built this overnight." *(screen recording)*
4. "My toddler just interrupted a build session. This is what building a startup as a dad actually looks like."
5. "People keep asking how I use AI agents. Here's the simplest explanation I can give."
6. "Here's the app I'm building and why it exists — in 30 seconds."
7. "I gave my AI agent a task before work. Here's what was waiting for me at 5pm."
8. "The hardest part of building a startup isn't the tech. It's the 2.5 hours."
9. "I only have 2.5 hours a day to work on my startup. Here's how I spend them."
10. "Someone asked me why I don't just quit my job. Here's the honest answer."
11. "Month 1 vs Month 3 of my app — built entirely by AI agents."
12. "My wife asked me to explain what I'm building. I recorded it."

One story. Twelve videos. Each one stands alone. Each one makes someone curious about the next.

### The Hook → Story → Payoff Structure

Every short-form video follows the same three-part structure:

**Hook (0-3 seconds):** The thing that stops the scroll. It's a bold claim, a surprising visual, or a relatable pain point. The hook is a *promise* — "if you keep watching, you'll see something interesting."

**Story (3-40 seconds):** The context, the journey, the "how." This is where you show, explain, or narrate. Keep it focused on one thing. If you feel yourself starting a new topic, that's a sign you need a second video.

**Payoff (last 5-10 seconds):** The moment that delivers on the hook's promise. A result, a reveal, a punchline, a realization. This is also where you plant the *curiosity gap* — the thing that makes someone follow you for what's next.

**Example using your story:**

- **Hook:** "I don't know how to code, but I'm building an app."
- **Story:** "I work a 9-to-5 at a startup and I have a toddler. I get maybe 2.5 hours a day to work on my own thing. So instead of learning to code, I built a team of AI agents. I give them tasks. They build features. I review what they made."
- **Payoff:** "This is what they built this week." *(show the app)* "Tomorrow I'll show you what happens when an agent gets it wrong." *(curiosity gap)*

### Curiosity Gaps

A curiosity gap is an open loop. You mention something interesting but don't resolve it. The viewer's brain wants closure, so they follow you. Examples:

- "I'll show you the biggest mistake my AI agent made in part 2."
- "Next week I'm launching the first version. Stay tuned."
- "But that's not even the craziest part — I'll tell you tomorrow."
- "I haven't told you about the time my agent deleted an entire feature. That's a story for another day."

Don't overdo this — one per video max, and only when it's natural. Forced cliffhangers feel manipulative.

### Reusable Video Templates

These are plug-and-play structures you can use over and over:

**Template 1: "Before Work / After Work"**
Hook: "I gave my AI agents a task before leaving for work."
Middle: Brief explanation of the task.
Payoff: Show what they built by the time you got home.

**Template 2: "People Don't Realize"**
Hook: "People don't realize you don't need to code to build an app in 2026."
Middle: Quick walkthrough of your process.
Payoff: Show the working product.

**Template 3: "Day in My Life"**
Hook: "What a day looks like when you have a 9-to-5, a toddler, and a startup."
Middle: Quick clips or narration over b-roll of your actual day.
Payoff: "And somehow, the app still got better today."

**Template 4: "The Honest Truth"**
Hook: "Here's what nobody tells you about building a startup as a parent."
Middle: Real talk about one specific struggle.
Payoff: Why you keep going anyway.

**Template 5: "I Tried X — Here's What Happened"**
Hook: "I asked an AI agent to build [specific feature]."
Middle: What you expected vs. what happened.
Payoff: The result (good or bad — both are content).

## 3. Content Pillars

Content pillars are recurring themes you rotate through. They keep your content varied enough to stay interesting but focused enough to build a recognizable brand. You have four.

### Pillar 1: The Build Journey
*"Look what's getting built"*

This is your flagship pillar. It's screen recordings, progress updates, milestone celebrations, and "look what my agents did" reveals.

- What the agents built this week
- Before/after of features
- App demos and walkthroughs
- Hitting milestones (first user, first paying customer, version launches)
- Bugs, failures, and things that went wrong (these often perform best)

**Primary audience:** Tech crowd, indie hackers, AI-curious people

### Pillar 2: The Dad/Founder Balance
*"The real life behind the build"*

This is the human, relatable content. The chaos of doing bedtime and then jumping on a laptop. The guilt of choosing work over play. The tiny wins that make it worth it.

- Morning routines and night routines
- Toddler interrupting your "work session"
- The 2.5-hour reality
- Conversations with your wife about the startup
- Why you're doing this for your family

**Primary audience:** Parents, working professionals with side projects, anyone who feels stretched thin

### Pillar 3: AI/Agent Education
*"Here's how this actually works"*

People are wildly curious about AI agents but most content about them is either too technical or too vague. You can be the person who explains it in plain English, from the perspective of someone who actually uses them to build a real product.

- "How I actually talk to my AI agents" (show prompts, show results)
- "What an AI agent can and can't do"
- "The tools I use to manage my AI team"
- Myth-busting: "No, the AI doesn't just magically build everything"
- Tips for other non-technical people who want to build with AI

**Primary audience:** AI-curious people, aspiring founders, non-technical builders

### Pillar 4: The Product
*"This is Kin and why it matters"*

This is where you show Kin itself — the morning briefing, the family coordination, the schedule management. The goal isn't a sales pitch. It's showing a real product solving a real problem in your real life.

- "Here's what Kin told me this morning" (morning briefing walkthrough)
- "How Kin keeps our family on track this week"
- Specific feature spotlights
- "I built this because nothing else worked for us"
- User reactions and feedback

**Primary audience:** Parents, families, people interested in productivity tools

### How the Pillars Rotate

A good weekly rhythm might look like this:

- Monday: Build Journey (what happened over the weekend / what's planned)
- Tuesday: AI Education (teach something you did recently)
- Wednesday: Dad/Founder Balance (real life moment)
- Thursday: Build Journey (mid-week progress)
- Friday: The Product (show Kin in action for the weekend)
- Saturday: Dad/Founder Balance or Product (lighter, weekend energy)

You don't have to be rigid about this. The point is variety. If you post five build updates in a row, the parent crowd tunes out. If you post five dad videos in a row, the tech crowd forgets you're building something.

## 4. Platform Strategy

### Where to Start: Pick ONE

You have 2.5 hours a day total for Kin. Content creation can't eat more than 3 hours per *week* or it'll cannibalize your build time. So start with one platform and do it well.

**Start with TikTok.** Here's why:

- The algorithm is the most generous to new creators. On TikTok, a video from someone with 0 followers can get 100K views if the content is good. Instagram and YouTube favor accounts that already have traction.
- Short-form video is the format (60 seconds or less), which means less editing, less production, less time.
- Your target demo (millennial parents, tech-curious people, aspiring founders) is very active on TikTok.
- The "raw and authentic" aesthetic that works best on TikTok is exactly what you'll be producing.

### Cross-Post Immediately

Once you're recording for TikTok, it takes approximately 2 minutes to also post to Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts. The video is already made. Just upload it to the other platforms.

- **Record on TikTok** (or record in your camera app and upload to TikTok)
- **Download without watermark** (use SnapTik or record natively in your camera app)
- **Upload the same video to Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts**

This is not "doing three platforms." It's doing one platform and copying the file to two other places. The algorithm on each platform will independently decide if it likes the video.

### Platform-Specific Notes

**TikTok:** Most forgiving for new creators. Text overlays and captions matter. Trending sounds can boost reach but aren't required for your niche. Longer videos (1-3 min) are getting more push in 2026.

**Instagram Reels:** More polished audience but still rewards authenticity. Your content will also live on your Instagram grid, which becomes a portfolio. Good for building credibility with the entrepreneur/startup crowd.

**YouTube Shorts:** Slower to gain traction but the audience is stickier. Good for the tech/AI education content. Also gives you a natural bridge to long-form YouTube later.

**Long-form YouTube (add later):** Once you have 30-60 days of short-form content and you know what resonates, start a YouTube channel with 8-15 minute videos. These could be weekly build logs, deeper AI tutorials, or "week in my life" vlogs. This is where the real community and monetization happens — but don't start here.

### Posting Cadence

**Realistic schedule:** 5 videos per week across all platforms (same video, posted to TikTok + Reels + Shorts).

If 5 feels like too much at first, do 3 per week. Consistency matters more than volume. Posting 3 videos every week for 3 months will outperform posting 7 videos in week 1 and then disappearing.

## 6. Video Production (Zero Budget)

### Equipment

Your iPhone. That's it. Seriously. The iPhone camera is better than what professional YouTubers were using five years ago. You don't need a ring light, a mic, or a gimbal to start. You might add those later, but they are not what's stopping you from posting.

### Recording Tips for Someone Who's Never Done This

**Start by talking to yourself.** Before you record your first real video, open your camera and talk for 60 seconds about your day. Don't post it. Just get used to the weird feeling of talking to a phone. Do this 3-4 times. By the 4th time, it'll feel less strange.

**Look at the camera lens, not the screen.** This is the #1 mistake new creators make. When you look at the screen, it looks like you're avoiding eye contact. The lens is the tiny dot at the top of your phone. Look at it.

**Talk slightly faster than feels natural.** On camera, your "normal" pace sounds slow. Speed up about 10-15%. This also helps with watch time because the viewer feels like the video is moving.

**Don't memorize a script.** Know your hook (the first sentence) and your payoff (the last sentence). Everything in the middle should be natural. If you mess up, keep going — you can trim in editing, or just leave it in. Nobody expects perfection.

**Record in 1080p vertical (9:16 aspect ratio).** If you're using your iPhone camera, just hold it vertically. TikTok, Reels, and Shorts are all vertical.

### The 5-Point Quick Setup

1. **Lighting:** Face a window. Natural light from the front is the single best thing you can do for video quality. Never have a window *behind* you (it makes you a silhouette). If it's dark, a $15 desk lamp pointed at the wall in front of you works great.

2. **Framing:** Head and shoulders visible. A little space above your head. Eyes roughly in the upper third of the frame. Lean your phone against something stable (a stack of books, a mug) or get a $10 phone tripod.

3. **Audio:** Record in a quiet room. Avoid rooms with hard floors and bare walls (echo). If you can, put a blanket or pillow behind your phone to reduce echo. Your iPhone mic is fine for now. If you want to upgrade one thing first, get a $20 lavalier mic that plugs into your phone.

4. **Background:** Keep it simple. A clean wall, a bookshelf, your desk setup. A slightly messy home office is fine — it's authentic. A disaster zone is distracting. Take 30 seconds to move anything weird out of frame.

5. **Wardrobe:** Wear what you actually wear. This isn't a photoshoot. Solid colors tend to look better on camera than busy patterns, but honestly, don't overthink it.

### Editing

**CapCut (free):** This is all you need. It's free, it's on your phone, and it can do everything: trim clips, add text overlays, add captions, add music, adjust speed. Spend 30 minutes watching one CapCut tutorial on YouTube and you'll know enough.

**Auto-captions:** Always add captions. A huge percentage of people watch without sound. TikTok and CapCut both have auto-caption features. Turn them on for every video.

**Keep edits simple.** Jump cuts (cutting out pauses and "ums") are the only editing technique you need for the first 30 days. Cut the dead air, add captions, maybe add a text overlay for your hook, and post it. That's it.

**How AI can help:** Use Claude or another AI to help you write hooks, outline video scripts, write captions, and brainstorm video ideas. You're already using AI to build — use it to create content too.

### The "Ugly But Authentic" Truth

Here's something counterintuitive: your first videos looking a little rough is actually an *advantage*. Polished content on TikTok and Reels often underperforms because it looks like an ad. People scroll past ads. People stop for real humans talking about real things.

The creator who films in their car after work will outperform the creator with a studio setup and a teleprompter — because the car video feels real. You're a dad filming in his home office at 9:30 PM. Lean into that. It's your aesthetic.

## 7. Common Mistakes for New Content Creators

### Trying to Go Viral on Video 1

Your first video will probably get 200 views. That's normal. That's good, actually. The algorithm is testing you with a small audience to see if they engage. If you go in expecting 100K views and get 200, you'll feel like a failure and quit. Reframe it: your first 10 videos are practice. You're learning the format, finding your voice, and letting the algorithm learn who your audience is.

### Over-Producing Content

If you spent 3 hours editing a 45-second video, something went wrong. For short-form content, the ROI on production quality drops off fast after "clear audio, decent lighting, captions." A video you agonize over for half a day will not perform meaningfully better than one you knocked out in 20 minutes. Post more, produce less.

### Waiting Until the Product Is "Ready"

The product will never be ready. If you wait until Kin is "done" to start posting, you'll start posting never. The build journey IS the content. The half-finished app IS interesting. People want to watch the house being built, not just see the finished house.

### Being Too Broad

"I make content about technology and life and startups and parenting and AI and productivity" — that's not a brand, that's a random person on the internet. You're the non-coding dad building a family app with AI agents. Every video should connect to some part of that identity. If a video doesn't, don't post it on this account.

### Idea Dumping

This is the thing you specifically flagged, and it's worth repeating: one video, one idea. If you're explaining your vision, you're not also explaining the tech stack. If you're showing a feature, you're not also talking about your daily routine. Separate them. Each idea gets its own video. You will feel like you're being too simple or too slow. You're not. You're being clear.

### Comparing Yourself to Established Creators

The creator with 500K followers who posts beautiful, effortless-looking content has been doing this for 3 years and has posted 1,500 videos. You're seeing their year-3 content and comparing it to your day-1 content. That comparison is meaningless. Go look at their first video — it probably looks just like yours will.

### Not Posting Because It's "Not Good Enough"

A video that gets posted and gets 50 views teaches you something. A video that sits in your drafts teaches you nothing. Done is better than perfect. Post it. The feedback loop only starts once you hit publish.

## 8. Growth Mechanics

### How the Algorithm Works

Every platform uses a similar system. When you post a video, it gets shown to a small test audience (usually 200-500 people). The algorithm measures how that group responds. Based on their response, it either shows the video to more people or stops pushing it.

The metrics that matter, roughly in order of importance:

1. **Watch time / completion rate** — Did people watch the whole video or scroll away? This is the #1 signal. A 30-second video where 80% of viewers watch all 30 seconds will outperform a 60-second video where people leave at second 15. This is why your hook matters so much — if people leave in the first 3 seconds, the video is dead.

2. **Shares** — Did people send this to a friend? Shares are the strongest engagement signal because they mean someone thought the video was worth another person's time.

3. **Saves** — Did people save this to watch again? Saves indicate high-value content. Educational content and "useful tips" get saved a lot.

4. **Comments** — Did people have something to say? Comments indicate the video sparked a reaction. Controversial (not toxic) takes and questions drive comments.

5. **Follows from the video** — Did people visit your profile and follow you after watching? This tells the algorithm your content creates fans, not just viewers.

### Hashtag Strategy

Hashtags matter less than they used to, but they still help the algorithm categorize your content. Use a mix:

- **2-3 broad hashtags:** #buildinpublic #startup #techstartup #ai
- **2-3 niche hashtags:** #nocode #aiagents #indiefounder #dadlife #founderstory
- **1 trending hashtag** (if relevant): Check what's trending on TikTok and use one only if it genuinely fits your video

Don't use 30 hashtags. Don't use hashtags that have nothing to do with your video. 5-7 targeted hashtags is the sweet spot.

### Engaging with Comments

This is where community actually gets built. For your first 90 days, reply to every single comment on your videos. Here's why it matters:

- Each reply counts as additional engagement, which tells the algorithm the video is generating conversation
- The person you reply to feels seen and becomes a loyal follower
- Comment threads make other viewers more likely to comment too
- You learn what your audience cares about (their questions become your next video ideas)

**Reply with personality.** Don't just say "thanks!" Ask a follow-up question. Be funny. Be real. The comments section is where followers become fans.

### Collaboration Opportunities

Once you have a small following (even 1,000 followers), look for collaboration:

- **Duet/stitch other creators** in the AI, startup, or parenting space
- **Reply to popular videos** in your niche with your own take
- **Tag and mention creators** whose work relates to yours (not in a spammy way — genuinely)
- **Guest appearances** on podcasts (lots of small podcasts need guests, and your story is interesting)

### Realistic Growth Timeline

- **Month 1:** 100-500 followers. Videos getting 200-2,000 views. This is normal. You're in "learning" mode.
- **Month 2-3:** 500-2,000 followers. You'll start to see which content types perform best. One or two videos might "pop" and get 10-50K views.
- **Month 3-6:** 2,000-10,000 followers. Consistency is paying off. You have a recognizable brand. People start referencing you in comments on other creators' videos.
- **Month 6-12:** 10,000-50,000+ followers. You're a known voice in the build-in-public / AI / founder space. Podcast invitations start coming. Potential for significant reach.

These numbers are estimates. Some people grow faster, some slower. The variable isn't talent — it's consistency and how well you dial in what your specific audience responds to.

## 9. From Followers to Customers

### The Funnel

Not everyone who follows you will become a Kin user, and that's fine. Here's how the funnel works:

**Viewer** → watches one video, maybe likes it
**Follower** → watches several videos, follows you for more
**Engaged follower** → comments, shares, feels connected to your journey
**Waitlist signup** → clicks the link in your bio, signs up to be notified
**Beta user** → tries Kin when you open access
**Paying customer** → sees enough value to pay

Your content serves the top of this funnel. Your product serves the bottom. Don't try to do both at the same time in every video.

### The 80/20 Rule

**80% of your content should be value and story.** Teach something. Share your journey. Be entertaining. Be relatable. Make people glad they followed you even if they never use Kin.

**20% of your content can be product-focused.** Show Kin, talk about what it does, invite people to try it. But even in these videos, lead with the value or the story, not the pitch.

If every video feels like a Kin commercial, people will unfollow. If every video is genuinely interesting and Kin shows up naturally, people will be curious about it on their own.

### When and How to Mention Kin

- **Always natural, never forced.** "This is what Kin showed me this morning" is natural. "Download Kin now, link in bio" in every video is forced.
- **Show it in use.** The best product marketing doesn't feel like marketing. Screen recordings of you actually using Kin as a dad are more persuasive than any pitch.
- **Once a week max for direct product content.** The rest of the time, Kin can be in the background — mentioned in passing, visible on your screen, part of the story — but not the main subject.

### Bio Optimization

Your bio is your storefront. When someone lands on your profile after watching a video, they read your bio and decide whether to follow. Keep it tight:

**Template:**
```
Dad | 9-to-5 | Building @KinAI with AI agents
No code. Just vibes and AI.
[Link to waitlist/landing page]
```

Your bio should reinforce your "one sentence." It should be scannable in 2 seconds. The link should go to one place — a waitlist page, a Linktree with your top 2-3 links max, or your website.

### Using Your Audience for Product Feedback

Your followers are a free focus group. Use them:

- **Ask questions in videos:** "What would you want a family coordination app to do? Comment below."
- **Run polls in Stories** (Instagram) about features
- **Show two options and ask which they prefer**
- **DM your most engaged followers** and ask for 5 minutes of feedback
- **Post screenshots of beta feedback** (with permission) — social proof

This creates a feedback loop: your audience shapes the product, which makes them more invested in it, which makes them more likely to become customers.

## 10. Austin's Weekly Content Playbook

### The System

This is the actual weekly workflow. Stick to it and content creation takes approximately 3 hours per week — well within your time budget.

### Sunday Evening: Plan (30 minutes)

- Review last week's analytics (which videos got the most views, comments, saves?)
- Pick 5 video topics for the week (use your content pillars to ensure variety)
- Write hooks for each video (just the first sentence)
- Jot 2-3 bullet points per video (not a full script — just what you want to hit)

### Monday Evening: Batch Record (60-75 minutes)

- Set up your phone (window light, leaning against something stable)
- Record 3-4 talking-head videos back to back
- For each: say the hook, talk through your bullet points, land the ending
- Don't aim for perfection — aim for "that's pretty good, next"
- Record any screen captures you need for the week

### Tuesday-Saturday: Post 1/Day (15 minutes each day)

- Edit the video for today (trim, add captions, add hook text overlay) — 10 min
- Write a caption (2-3 sentences + hashtags) — 2 min
- Post to TikTok, then cross-post to Reels and Shorts — 3 min
- Respond to comments from yesterday's video while you're in the apps

### Throughout the Week: Capture Moments

- Keep your phone handy for "in-the-moment" content
- When something interesting happens with the build, your kid, or your day — record a quick 15-second clip
- These spontaneous clips become some of your best-performing content
- Save them in a "content" album on your phone

### The Weekly Rhythm at a Glance

| Day | Activity | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Sunday | Plan next week's content, review analytics | 30 min |
| Monday | Batch record 3-4 videos | 60-75 min |
| Tuesday | Edit + post video 1, reply to comments | 15 min |
| Wednesday | Edit + post video 2, reply to comments | 15 min |
| Thursday | Edit + post video 3, reply to comments | 15 min |
| Friday | Edit + post video 4, reply to comments | 15 min |
| Saturday | Edit + post video 5 (or rest), reply to comments | 15 min |
| **Total** | | **~3 hrs/week** |

### Quick-Reference: Video Checklist

Before you post any video, run through this:

- [ ] Does it have a strong hook in the first 3 seconds?
- [ ] Is it about ONE idea (not three)?
- [ ] Would someone who's never seen my content understand it?
- [ ] Does it connect to my "one sentence" (dad + 9-to-5 + no code + AI agents + app)?
- [ ] Are captions on?
- [ ] Is there a curiosity gap or call to action at the end?
- [ ] Have I added 5-7 relevant hashtags?
