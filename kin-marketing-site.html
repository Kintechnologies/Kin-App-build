What kin watches for

Six moments where
coordination breaks down.

Kin watches your household for exactly these situations — and surfaces the implication before you feel the impact. Not data. Not a notification you have to interpret. A clear answer.

Pickup Risk
Coverage is unclear when a child needs to be somewhere.
Kin checks both calendars against every pickup window. If one or both parents are blocked, it names who's responsible — or escalates if nobody is.
"Your partner's tied up — pickup's yours tonight. I'll remind you when it's time to leave."
Schedule Compression
Required events stack up with no margin between them.
Pickup → activity → dinner → bedtime. Kin calculates the chain, adds travel time, and flags when the window is too tight for a family to absorb without a plan.
"Practice ends at 7, bedtime at 8:30. That's an 80-minute window for dinner, bath, and wind-down. Tight."
Late Schedule Change
A calendar update lands mid-day and creates a new coordination problem.
Kin monitors calendars in real time. When something changes that affects a pickup, an activity, or a household responsibility — you know in seconds, not at 5pm.
"Your partner's 6pm moved to 7:30 — you've got pickup tonight."
Responsibility Shift
One parent's plans change in a way that transfers a household task to the other.
Kin knows who typically handles what. When the default handler goes unavailable, Kin tells the other parent — before they find out the hard way.
"You've got a late meeting — your partner should know about dinner."
Coverage Gap
An activity or commitment has no parent assigned and time is running out.
Different from a pickup conflict — this is the commitment that slips through before anyone checks. Kin flags it at 48 hours so there's still time to sort it.
"Soccer practice ends at 5:30 Thursday — nobody's on pickup for that yet."
Transition Risk
A work event ends too close to a household responsibility to make it without rushing.
When you have less than 15 minutes between your last meeting and pickup, Kin tells you — even if it's technically possible. Technically possible isn't the standard.
"Your meeting ends at 5:45 and pickup's at 6. Tight — heads up."
Escalation

Kin doesn't send one message
and move on.

Every coordination issue is tracked. As time collapses toward an event, the urgency increases — and so does the directness. Kin is alongside you, not behind you.

T-6 hours · Morning briefing
Planning note.
You have time.
Surfaces in the morning briefing as a heads-up. Future-tense. Informational — not alarming. You know what's coming before the day starts.
"Your partner has a late meeting tonight — pickup will probably fall on you."
T-2 hours · Push notification
Clear implication.
Act now.
Direct push notification. Present-tense. The situation is real and you need to know what it means for the next two hours — not just that something exists.
"Your partner's meeting runs until 6. Pickup's on you — leave by 5:40."
T-45 minutes · High-priority alert
One sentence.
Leave now.
Elevated push. Imperative. No hedging, no explanation. At this point, one thing matters — getting out the door. Kin says exactly that.
"Leave now — pickup in 45 minutes."
When nothing needs your attention

Silence is a feature,
not a gap.

Most apps fill silence with noise. Kin only speaks when the output is worth saying. When your day is clear, Kin tells you that too — and then stays quiet.

The clean day output
When nothing needs coordination, Kin says so. Then stops.
Kin has a hard rule: if an output wouldn't change what you do, it shouldn't exist. Vague insights — "looks like a busy week" — don't get surfaced. If Kin has nothing real to say, it either confirms clean or says nothing at all. That restraint is what makes the alerts that do come through worth listening to.
"Clean day — nothing to stay ahead of."
— Kin, on a good day
How it works

Set up in minutes.
First insight by morning.

Kin doesn't need weeks to calibrate. Connect your calendars, tell it about your household, and it starts watching. Most families get a real coordination insight on day one.

01 — CONNECT
Link your calendars and invite your partner

Connect Google or Apple Calendar — both parents. Invite your partner. Tell Kin about your kids, their schedules, and who handles what. Through conversation, not a form.

02 — LEARN
Kin builds your household model

Both calendars merge. Kin maps pickup defaults, evening routines, recurring commitments. The first 48 hours are spent filling gaps so every insight after that is high-confidence.

03 — WATCH
Kin tracks it so you don't have to

From there, Kin runs quietly in the background — watching for conflicts, shifts, and gaps. When something needs your attention, it tells you what it means and what to do.

The architecture

Two private profiles.
One family view.

Every other family app gives you one shared login. Kin gives each parent their own private AI and private calendar — and merges them into a shared household layer where it matters.
