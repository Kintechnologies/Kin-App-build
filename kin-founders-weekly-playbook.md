# Kin AI — Founder's Weekly Playbook

*The operating manual for building a company in 2.5 hours a day.*

---

You work a 9-to-5, in-office five days a week. You have six AI agents doing the execution. Your job is to be the face, the voice, the decision-maker, and the person who talks to users. Everything else is delegation.

This playbook tells you exactly what to do with your time — morning, lunch, evening, weekend — across three phases: getting to beta, surviving launch, and growing into something fundable.

Read this Sunday night. Follow it Monday morning. Adjust as you learn.

---

## 1. The Time Budget

### Weekday Anatomy

| Block | Time | Duration | What It's For |
|-------|------|----------|---------------|
| Morning | 6:30 – 7:45 AM | 75 min | Deep work: approvals, strategy, content recording |
| Commute | ~8:00 AM | 15 min | Audio: listen to user voice notes, think through decisions |
| Lunch | 12:00 – 12:30 PM | 30 min | Quick approvals, user DMs, community check-in |
| Commute home | ~5:30 PM | 15 min | Audio review, mental debrief |
| Evening | 7:30 – 9:00 PM | 90 min | Dispatch review, content approval, strategic thinking |

**Weekday total: ~3.5 hours** (75 + 30 + 90 min active, plus ~30 min commute thinking)

But not all of that is equal. The morning 75 minutes is your highest-leverage block — protect it ruthlessly. No email. No scrolling. No "quick checks." This is where strategy happens.

### Weekend Anatomy

| Block | Time | Duration | What It's For |
|-------|------|----------|---------------|
| Saturday morning | 7:00 – 9:00 AM | 120 min | Weekly review, batch content recording, strategic thinking |
| Sunday evening | 8:00 – 9:30 PM | 90 min | Week preview, content approval batch, set Monday priority |

**Weekend total: ~3.5 hours**

### Weekly Grand Total: ~21 hours

That's a part-time job on top of your full-time job. It's enough — but only if every hour is intentional.

## 2. Pre-Launch Phase Playbook (Now → Beta)

*You're here. TestFlight in ~11 days. Every minute counts.*

### The One Rule

**Your agents are building. You are recruiting and recording.**

The build sprint is running. Your six agents have their tasks. You don't write code. You don't debug. You don't format documents. You approve, you recruit beta testers, and you record content so you have assets ready for launch day.

### Weekly Schedule

#### Monday

| Lunch (30 min) | DM 3-5 people from yesterday's community post. "Hey, I'm building something for exactly this problem. Can I show you in 2 weeks?" | 30 min |
| Evening (90 min) | Review QA agent's test results. Review design specs from Product & Design agent. Approve or send back with one-line notes. | 90 min |

#### Wednesday

| Block | Task | Time |
|-------|------|------|
| Morning (75 min) | Apple Developer setup tasks: App Store listing copy review, screenshots plan, TestFlight configuration. Approve agent-drafted descriptions. | 75 min |

(4) Review and respond to any beta tester DMs — 20 min. | 90 min |

### Pre-Launch Priorities (Ranked)

1. **Beta tester recruitment** — nothing else matters if nobody uses it
2. **Build approvals** — unblock your agents; a blocked agent is a wasted day
3. **Content recording** — bank 10-15 raw videos before launch day
4. **Apple Developer setup** — don't let bureaucracy be the bottleneck
5. **Community presence** — plant seeds now, harvest at launch

### What Agents Handle Autonomously (Don't Touch These)

- All code writing and PR creation
- QA test execution and bug filing
- Marketing copy first drafts
- Research summaries
- Design spec iterations (until final review)
- Sprint task breakdown and scheduling
- Documentation formatting

### What Needs Your Approval

- Any copy that goes public (social posts, App Store listing, website)
- Design direction changes (not iterations — direction)
- Spending over $0 (yes, zero — you approve all spending right now)
- Build milestone sign-offs ("yes, this is ready for TestFlight")
- Beta tester communication templates

## 3. Launch Phase Playbook (First 30 Days with Users)

*People are using your app. This changes everything.*

### The One Rule

**Talk to users every single day. Not surveys. Conversations.**

Your morning briefing tells you what the app knows. Your user conversations tell you what the app doesn't know. The gap between those two things is your entire product roadmap.

### Daily Non-Negotiables

| Task | When | Time | Why |
|------|------|------|-----|
| Read your own Kin morning briefing | 6:30 AM, first thing | 5 min | Eat your own cooking. If it's not useful to you, it's not useful to anyone. |
| Check Dispatch approval queue | Morning block | 15 min | Unblock agents. Fast approvals keep the machine running. |
| DM 2-3 users | Lunch + evening | 20 min total | "How was your briefing this morning?" "What did Kin get wrong?" "What did you expect that wasn't there?" |
| Review crash/error reports | Evening | 10 min | QA agent flags these. You decide severity and priority. |

### Weekly Schedule (Launch Phase)

#### Monday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning | Read briefing. Set weekly priority. Review weekend user feedback (agent-compiled summary). |
| Lunch | DM 3 users. Focus on anyone in their first 3 days — the onboarding experience is everything right now. |
| Evening | Review metrics dashboard: DAU, briefing open rate, Day 1→Day 2 retention. Approve content queue for the week. |

#### Tuesday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning | Record 1 short-form video. Show the real app on your real phone. "Day [X] of launch, here's what we're seeing." |
| Lunch | User conversations. Focus on anyone who hasn't opened their briefing in 2+ days — what happened? |
| Evening | Review agent sprint outputs. Approve bug fixes. Prioritize feature requests from user conversations. |

#### Wednesday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning | Mid-week check-in. Review: are we retaining? Look at the 7-day cohort. How many Day 1 users came back on Day 7? |
| Lunch | User conversations. This time: talk to the power users. What do they love? What would make them tell a friend? |
| Evening | Content approval batch. Review everything agents drafted. Approve or flag. |

#### Thursday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning | Record 1 video. This one can be a user story (with permission): "One of our families told us ___." |
| Lunch | Beta tester outreach. Anyone who hasn't been active in 5+ days gets a personal DM. Not automated. You, personally. |
| Evening | Review trial→paid conversion data. How many free trials converted? What's the pattern? |

#### Friday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning | Light approval morning. Clear the queue. |
| Lunch | Community engagement. Share a genuine insight from the week. Not marketing — learning. |
| Evening | Weekly metrics review. Compile: DAU, retention curves, conversion rate, NPS/qualitative themes. Write a 3-line summary for yourself. |

#### Saturday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Morning (120 min) | Batch: (1) Weekly report review — 20 min. (2) Record 2 content pieces — 40 min. (3) Deep user feedback analysis — what's the #1 thing users want that we don't have? — 30 min. (4) Strategic thinking: is the current trajectory hitting 20 DAU families in 30 days? — 30 min. |

#### Sunday

| Block | Task |
|-------|------|
| Evening (90 min) | Week preview. Approve content batch. Plan Monday priority. DM any users who reached out over the weekend. |

### Metrics to Obsess Over

| Metric | Target | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Daily Active Families | 20 for 30 consecutive days | This is your proof of retention. Nothing else matters until this is hit. |
| Morning Briefing Open Rate | >70% | If people open the briefing, Kin is part of their routine. If not, you lose. |
| Day 1 → Day 7 Retention | >40% | Industry benchmark for good consumer apps. Below 30% = onboarding problem. |
| Trial → Paid Conversion | >15% | At $39/month with a 7-day trial, you need strong conversion. Below 10% = value problem. |
| Partner Invite Rate | >50% | Kin works best with both parents. If only one signs up, the intelligence is half-powered. |

### What to Ignore (For Now)

- Total downloads (vanity metric — retention is what matters)
- Social media follower counts
- Feature requests from non-users
- Competitor announcements
- App Store ranking
- Press coverage opportunities (too early)
- Anything that doesn't directly answer: "Are families coming back every day?"

### How to Talk to Your First 20 Beta Families

**The approach:** You're not customer support. You're not doing research. You're having conversations with people whose lives you're trying to make easier.

**Opening messages (pick what feels natural):**
- "Hey [name], I saw you connected your calendar yesterday — how was your first briefing this morning?"
- "Quick question — when you saw [specific thing in their briefing], was that actually useful or just noise?"
- "I noticed you haven't opened the app in a couple days — totally fine, just curious if something felt off?"

**What to listen for:**
- The words they use (adopt their language in your marketing)
- The moment they say "oh, I wish it could ___" (that's your roadmap)
- Frustration (that's a bug or onboarding gap)
- Delight (that's your marketing message)

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't ask "would you pay for this?" (they're already in a paid trial — watch what they do)
- Don't ask "what features do you want?" (too abstract — watch what they struggle with)
- Don't send surveys (conversations > surveys, always)

## 4. Post-Launch Phase Playbook (Month 2+)

*You have retention data. Now what?*

### When to Approach Investors

**Not yet.** Here's when:

You need a story that goes: "20 families used Kin every day for 30 days. X% converted to paid. Here's what they said. Here's what we're building next. Here's why this is a $100M+ market."

That story requires data you don't have yet. Get the data first.

### What the Pitch Deck Needs to Show

Investors at the pre-seed/seed stage for a consumer app want to see:

1. **Retention curve that flattens** — not just "we got users" but "they stayed." Show a 30-day cohort chart where the line flattens above 30%. This is the single most important slide.
2. **Unit economics that work** — $39/month × conversion rate × average lifetime. If LTV > 3× CAC, you have a business.
3. **User quotes that make you feel something** — not "it's nice" but "I can't believe we coordinated our whole week in 60 seconds." Emotion sells at this stage.
4. **The wedge** — calendar coordination is the entry point, but the vision is a full family OS across 11 life domains. Show the expansion path.
5. **Why AI-native wins** — Kin isn't a calendar app with AI bolted on. The intelligence IS the product. The app is just the surface.
6. **Solo founder + AI team as a feature** — you built this with 6 AI agents. That's not a weakness; it's a thesis on the future of company building. Lean into it.

### Partnership Outreach

**Influencers (Month 2-3):**
- Target: parenting content creators with 10K-100K followers (micro-influencers convert better)
- Offer: free lifetime access + their audience gets an extended 14-day trial
- Approach: DM, not email. Be a human. "I built this because my wife and I couldn't keep our schedules straight. I think your audience deals with the same thing."
- Let agents draft outreach templates; you personalize and send

**Apple App Store Featuring (Month 3+):**
- Requires: polished App Store listing, strong ratings (4.5+), consistent updates
- The angle: "AI-native family coordination" — Apple loves showcasing AI-forward apps
- Submit to Apple's editorial team via App Store Connect
- Your agents can research the submission process and draft the pitch

### Hiring First Help

**When:** You have 50+ paying subscribers and you're drowning in user conversations (a good problem)

**Who first:** A part-time community manager / customer success person. NOT an engineer. Your agents handle engineering. You need someone to:
- Respond to user DMs during your work hours (9-5)
- Compile user feedback themes for your evening review
- Moderate any community spaces
- Handle TestFlight onboarding logistics

**Budget:** $500-800/month part-time. That's 15-20 paying subscribers' worth. Wait until you have 3× that in revenue.

### When to Consider Leaving the 9-5

This is the big one. Don't romanticize it. Here's the decision framework:

| Signal | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Monthly recurring revenue | >$5,000/month (128+ subscribers) | You can survive (barely) without the day job |
| Revenue trend | Growing >15% month-over-month for 3+ months | This isn't a fluke |
| Time constraint | You're turning away users or missing growth opportunities because you can't respond fast enough | The 9-5 is now costing you money |
| Fundraise ready | You have a term sheet or strong verbal commitment | Someone else believes in this enough to fund it |

**The real answer:** You leave when NOT leaving is more expensive than leaving. Until then, the 9-5 is your runway. It's not a limitation — it's what lets you take risks without starving.

## 6. The "Never Do This" List

If you catch yourself doing any of these, stop immediately. You're spending founder time on agent work.

- **Writing code.** Not a single line. Not "just a quick fix." File a task for the Lead Engineer agent.
- **Writing marketing copy from scratch.** Brand & Growth agent drafts. You approve or edit. You never start from blank.
- **Doing research.** You don't Google competitors. You don't read industry reports. Your Research agent reads them and gives you a summary. You read the summary.
- **Formatting documents.** If it needs to look pretty, that's an agent task.
- **Managing agent schedules.** Chief of Staff coordinates the agents. You set priorities, not schedules.
- **Debugging builds.** QA agent files the bug. Lead Engineer agent fixes it. You verify the fix works in the app.
- **Designing UI/UX.** Product & Design agent handles this. You give feedback on what they produce.
- **Editing videos.** Record raw. Agents edit. You approve the final cut.
- **Responding to generic support questions.** Create FAQ templates. Agents handle routine questions. You handle the conversations that require a human.
- **Optimizing ad spend.** (When you get there.) Agents run experiments. You approve budgets and review results.

**The test:** Before you start any task, ask: "Is this something only a human founder can do, or am I doing it because it feels productive?" If it's the second one, delegate it.

## 7. Decision Framework

When a Dispatch notification hits your phone, you have three options. Pick one in under 60 seconds.

### ✅ Approve and Move On (< 30 seconds)

**Use when:**
- The output matches what you expected
- It's an iteration, not a new direction
- The agent has done this type of work before and you trust the pattern
- It's internal (not user-facing)

**How:** Glance. Does it look right? Approve. Move on. Don't wordsmith internal docs.

### 🔄 Flag for Revision (< 2 minutes)

**Use when:**
- The direction is right but the execution needs adjustment
- There's a specific, articulable issue ("the tone is too formal" / "this metric is wrong" / "swap section 2 and 3")
- You can describe the fix in one sentence

**How:** Write one line of feedback. Be specific. "Make the CTA more direct" is better than "this doesn't feel right." Send it back. Don't rewrite it yourself.

### 🛑 Block and Discuss (Save for evening deep work)

**Use when:**
- The output reveals a strategic misalignment
- You need to rethink the approach, not just the execution
- The decision has downstream consequences you need to think through
- It involves spending money or public-facing commitments

**How:** Star it. Don't try to resolve it in 30 seconds during lunch. Put it in your evening deep work block. Think it through. Then send clear direction.

### The Ratio

In a healthy week, your approvals should be roughly: 70% approve instantly, 20% flag for revision, 10% block for discussion. If you're blocking more than 20%, your agents need better briefs. If you're approving 95%+, you might not be reviewing critically enough.

## 8. Weekly Rituals

These are the anchors of your week. They don't move. Everything else flexes around them.

### Sunday Evening (8:00 – 9:30 PM) — "The Setup"

1. **Review the content queue** (30 min) — Agents have been drafting all week. Approve the batch so everything is ready to publish Monday through Friday. This is your biggest content decision of the week.
2. **Preview next week's sprint** (20 min) — What are agents working on? Any decisions needed before they start Monday? Leave clear notes.
3. **Write your Monday priority** (5 min) — One sentence. "This week, the most important thing is ___." This is the lens through which you evaluate everything else.
4. **Respond to weekend user messages** (20 min) — Anyone who reached out gets a reply before the week starts. No one should wait until Monday afternoon.
5. **Personal check-in** (15 min) — How are you feeling about this? What's your energy level? Be honest with yourself. Adjust the week's intensity if needed.

### Monday Morning (6:30 – 7:45 AM) — "The Kickoff"

1. **Read your own Kin briefing** (5 min) — Are you eating your own cooking?
2. **Review Dispatch overnight outputs** (30 min) — Approve what's ready. Flag what's not.
3. **Send the weekly priority to your agents** (5 min) — One sentence. Clear. Non-negotiable.
4. **Quick scan of user metrics** (10 min) — Any red flags from the weekend?

### Wednesday Lunch (12:00 – 12:30 PM) — "The Pulse Check"

1. **User conversations** (15 min) — DM 2-3 people. How is their week going with Kin?
2. **Mid-week sprint check** (15 min) — Are we on track? Any blockers that need your decision?

### Friday Evening (7:30 – 9:00 PM) — "The Review"

1. **Metrics review** (30 min) — DAU, retention, conversion, briefing open rate. Write 3 bullet points: what's working, what's not, what to try next week.
2. **Clear the approval queue** (30 min) — Don't leave agents blocked over the weekend.
3. **Weekend planning** (15 min) — What's the one thing to batch on Saturday morning?
4. **User message cleanup** (15 min) — Reply to anyone you missed during the week.

### Saturday Morning (7:00 – 9:00 AM) — "The Batch"

1. **Weekly report deep-read** (20 min) — Chief of Staff agent compiles this. Read it carefully. This is your state-of-the-company briefing.
2. **Content recording** (40 min) — Batch 2-3 videos. Saturday morning light is good. You're relaxed. It shows on camera.
3. **Strategic thinking** (30 min) — No notifications. No approvals. Just you and the question: "What's the biggest risk to Kin right now, and what do I do about it?"
4. **Personal outreach** (15 min) — Text 3 people. Friends, family, people you met at events. "Hey, I'm building this thing — can I show you?"

## 9. Key Milestones & Decision Points

### "Ready for Beta Testers"

| Signal | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Core flow works end-to-end | User can: sign up → connect calendar → get first briefing within 24 hours |
| Partner invite works | Second parent can join and their calendar merges into the household |
| Briefing quality is good enough | You read your own briefing 3 mornings in a row and it was useful each time |
| Crash rate | < 1% of sessions |
| Beta list size | 20+ people who've said "yes" |

**The gut check:** Would you be embarrassed if a friend used this tomorrow? If yes, it's not ready. If "no, but it's rough" — ship it. Rough is fine. Broken is not.

### "Ready for Public Launch"

| Signal | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Beta retention | 40%+ of beta users active after 14 days |
| Critical bugs | Zero known crashes or data-loss bugs |
| Onboarding completion rate | 70%+ of signups complete calendar connection |
| Payment flow | Stripe live, tested with real card, subscription works end-to-end |
| App Store listing | Approved and live (even if you keep it unlisted initially) |
| Content bank | 10+ pieces ready to publish in first 2 weeks |

**The gut check:** If 100 strangers downloaded this tomorrow, would 30+ of them get value from it? If yes, launch. Perfection is the enemy.

### "Ready to Talk to Investors"

| Signal | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Daily active families | 20+ for 30 consecutive days |
| Paid subscribers | 50+ |
| Monthly recurring revenue | $1,500+/month |
| Retention curve | Flattening (not still dropping) at day 30 |
| User quotes | 5+ unsolicited "I love this" messages you can screenshot |
| Clear growth lever | You know what channel acquires users and roughly what it costs |

**The gut check:** Can you tell a 3-minute story with real data that makes an investor think "this could be big"? If you're hedging, you need more data.

### "Ready to Leave the 9-5"

| Signal | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Monthly recurring revenue | $5,000+/month (sustainable) |
| Revenue trajectory | 15%+ MoM growth for 3+ consecutive months |
| Runway | 6+ months of personal expenses saved OR funding secured |
| Opportunity cost | You can point to specific growth you're missing because of the day job |
| Family alignment | Your wife is on board. This is a joint decision. |

**The gut check:** If this fails in 12 months and you need to find another job, will you be okay? If the answer is "yes, and I'd regret not trying" — it's time. If the answer involves the word "hopefully" — wait.

## The Meta-Rule

Every week, ask yourself one question:

**"Did I spend my time this week on things only I can do?"**

If yes, you're operating correctly. If you spent 3 hours on something an agent could have done, that's 3 hours you didn't spend talking to users or recording content.

Your agents are your team. Trust them with execution. Save yourself for the things that require a human heartbeat: being seen, being heard, making judgment calls, and building relationships that compound.

The company gets built in 2.5 hours a day. But only if those hours are the right hours.
